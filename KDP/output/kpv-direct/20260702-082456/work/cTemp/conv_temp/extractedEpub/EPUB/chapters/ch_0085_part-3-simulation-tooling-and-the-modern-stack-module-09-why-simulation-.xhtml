Section 9.5: Benchmark environment map

"A benchmark is a contract. Sign it the same way every time, or the results are not comparable."

A Referee Environment, Strictly

This section assumes familiarity with the reality gap and transfer metrics introduced in section 9.4. The benchmark selection discipline developed here is applied directly in section 10.1, where Gymnasium APIs enforce the observation, action, and reset contracts described on this page, and in section 12.5, where Habitat, AI2-THOR, and ProcTHOR are evaluated as concrete benchmark choices. The sim-to-real robustness construct used as a running example recurs in section 20.1 alongside domain randomization and hardware calibration methods.

Big Picture

Two teams report state-of-the-art grasping (as of 2024). Both claim 90 % success. Neither can compare results because one used MuJoCo with a parallel gripper and the other used IsaacGym with a dexterous hand, different contact solvers, different reset seeds, different success thresholds. The field now has dozens of simulators, each freezing a different slice of physics and semantics, and picking the wrong one invalidates a claim before the robot is ever touched.

This section maps the benchmark landscape so you can match the environment to the construct you actually need to test. Figure 9.5A places the major simulators on axes of embodiment type and task complexity, giving you a quick visual entry point before the selection discipline below. You will leave with a principled way to audit any simulator choice and expose the failure modes that matter for your specific embodied agent.

Benchmarks Are Constructs, Not Leaderboards

Ask yourself: when a leaderboard says a grasping policy scores 91%, ninety-one percent of what, measured by whose contact solver, on which object meshes, under what success threshold? A benchmark environment never measures "grasping" in the abstract; it measures one frozen construct: locomotion stability, contact-rich manipulation, multi-agent coordination, navigation under partial observability, household task completion, visual grounding, or sim-to-real robustness. The benchmark's application programming interface (API), assets, physics, sensor model, and metric define that construct, and the score is meaningless until you can name it.

The practical question is not which environment is most popular. The question is which environment makes the right failure possible. Consider one documented case. A team scored 91% on a manipulation benchmark, then saw only 40% success on the real robot. The benchmark's contact model did not reproduce the target gripper surface. The same policy and the same hardware produced a 51-percentage-point drop, which makes the reality gap measurable and traces it entirely to a missing construct. This is called the construct-validity gap, and, in practice, a benchmark comparison that ignores it is typically comparing noise rather than a genuine method difference. A policy that scores well in a simulator whose construct does not match the real task is not a validated policy: it is a well-rehearsed assumption. The team lacked a construct-matched friction probe, so it ran 50,000 rollouts confirming a score the real robot could never reproduce. A single 300-episode probe on the calibrated construct would have surfaced the failure before any policy tuning began. A grasping benchmark that never models contact slip cannot validate contact robustness. A household benchmark without persistent object state cannot validate long-horizon interaction. Figure 9.5B traces this reasoning as a flow: a claim's required features are matched against each environment's coverage, producing either a candidate environment or a logged claim limit.

Think of a benchmark like a standardized recipe judging: the judges score only the dish you plated, not whether you can cook the dish a different way tomorrow. A team that perfects the exact recipe for the judges (contact model, friction coefficients, object meshes) will win the competition score, but that score says nothing about cooking the same meal with different ingredients in a real kitchen. The construct-validity gap is exactly this: the judges froze a specific set of conditions, and your policy learned to win those frozen conditions, not the broader skill you intended to prove.

Choose For The Failure You Need To See

The best benchmark is the one whose task contract can expose the failure mode that would invalidate your claim. A high score on the wrong construct is not evidence for the real robot you want to build.

Environment Families And Valid Claims

Gymnasium-style APIs are useful for clean reinforcement-learning contracts. PettingZoo extends that discipline to multi-agent settings. MuJoCo, robosuite, ManiSkill, and Isaac Lab are often chosen for contact, manipulation, locomotion, vectorized rollouts, and controller integration. Habitat, AI2-THOR, ProcTHOR, BEHAVIOR, and OmniGibson emphasize embodied navigation, household semantics, object state, and visual interaction.

Each family has a validity envelope. If the claim is about torque-level transfer, check dynamics, contacts, actuation, and timing. If the claim is about embodied household reasoning, check scene diversity, affordances, persistent state, and task definitions. If the claim is about policy comparison, check whether all methods run through the same wrapper and metric script.

Mechanism

A benchmark works by freezing enough of the world contract that two policies can be compared. For a ManiSkill pick-and-place panel the frozen pieces are concrete: the reset distribution (object pose sampled in a 20 cm tray region), the observation space (RGB-D from a fixed wrist camera plus 7-DoF Franka joint proprioception), the action space (end-effector delta pose, where the end-effector is the gripper or tool mounted at the tip of the robot arm), the time limit (200 sim steps at 100 Hz), the success metric (object lifted above 2 cm and held), the contact solver and friction coefficients, and the logging format. Any unfrozen piece, a different gripper pad, a re-seeded object mesh, a changed friction default, should be recorded as an experimental degree of freedom rather than silently absorbed into the score.

Worked Example

missing = sorted(claim_needs - supports)

Step-Through: Construct Matching For A Grasping Claim

Trace the coverage check with concrete values. The claim is "vision-conditioned contact grasping with labeled failures," so the required feature set is R(C) = {contact, object_state, vision, failure_labels}, four features total.

MuJoCo manipulation supports {contact, controller_timing, failure_labels}. Intersection with R(C) = {contact, failure_labels}, so coverage s = 2/4 = 0.50. Gap = {object_state, vision}. Both are non-negotiable for a vision claim, so it is discarded and logged in the known-limit set.

ManiSkill supports {contact, vision, object_state, failure_labels}. Intersection = all four, so s = 4/4 = 1.00. Gap = {} (empty). It becomes a candidate.

Habitat navigation supports {vision, layout_diversity, navigation_metrics}. Intersection = {vision}, so s = 1/4 = 0.25. Gap = {contact, object_state, failure_labels}. Discarded.

ProcTHOR household supports {vision, object_state, layout_diversity}. Intersection = {vision, object_state}, so s = 2/4 = 0.50. Gap = {contact, failure_labels}. Discarded.

Ranked by s descending: ManiSkill (1.00) is the sole survivor; the other three tie or trail and all fail the non-negotiable check. The winning candidate is ManiSkill with a fully covered construct.

Checkpoint

So far: a claim's required features form a set R(C); each candidate environment's supported features form F(e); coverage is the overlap between the two, and a missing non-negotiable feature discards the candidate for that claim (as the worked example just showed for ManiSkill versus MuJoCo, Habitat, and ProcTHOR). The algorithm below formalizes that same coverage check into a repeatable selection procedure.

Algorithm: Benchmark Environment Selection via Construct Matching

Input: claim construct $C$, candidate environment set $\mathcal{E} = \{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$, each with feature set $F(e_i)$; required feature set $R(C)$; transfer probe budget $\pi$

Output: ranked candidate list $\mathcal{E}^* \subseteq \mathcal{E}$, benchmark card $\theta$ (a written record of the environment's frozen contract, defined in full under "Write The Card Before The Policy" below), known-limit set $\Lambda$

  1. Express claim $C$ as a set of required features $R(C)$ covering observation space $o$, action space $a$, success metric $m$, failure labels $\ell$, and asset distribution $\alpha$.
  2. For each candidate $e_i \in \mathcal{E}$, compute the coverage gap $\Delta_i = R(C) \setminus F(e_i)$ and score coverage $s_i = |R(C) \cap F(e_i)| / |R(C)|$.
  3. Discard any $e_i$ where $\Delta_i$ contains a non-negotiable feature (contact model, object persistence, or failure label type); record each discard in $\Lambda$.
  4. Rank remaining $\mathcal{E}^*$ by $s_i$ descending; break ties by asset diversity and maintained wrapper quality.
  5. For the top-ranked $e^* \in \mathcal{E}^*$, instantiate benchmark card $\theta$ with fields: environment version, observation space $o$, action space $a$, reset distribution $\rho$, seed set $\mathcal{S}$, metric $m$, failure taxonomy $\ell$, and known limit $\Lambda$.
  6. Run a random policy $\pi_{\text{rand}}$ and a scripted baseline $\pi_{\text{base}}$ through the same evaluation wrapper; verify reset integrity, metric consistency, and absence of privileged-state leakage.
  7. Freeze the held-out task panel $\mathcal{T}_{\text{held}}$ before any policy $\pi$ is tuned; record the frozen panel hash in $\theta$.
  8. Execute policy $\pi$ under $\theta$; log per-episode traces, $\nabla$ reward signals, failure labels, and configuration artifact.
  9. For any real-world transfer claim, perform at least one hardware probe covering the target object set; annotate gap $\nabla_{\text{real}} = \theta_{\text{sim}} - \theta_{\text{real}}$ in $\Lambda$.
  10. Report three separate claims: benchmark claim (what $e^*$ measures), systems claim (what artifact makes results reproducible), and transfer claim (what $\Lambda$ leaves untested).
Library Shortcut

The hand checklist is for understanding. In practice, Gymnasium, PettingZoo, ManiSkill, Isaac Lab, Habitat, and ProcTHOR expose maintained wrappers, seed handling, task registries, and metric scripts. The shortcut is valuable only if the wrapper preserves the benchmark's validity contract instead of hiding it.

Practical Recipe

  1. Write the claim as a construct: contact robustness, visual navigation, household state tracking, coordination, or transfer.
  2. Choose an environment family whose observation, action, asset, reset, and metric contract can measure that construct.
  3. Run a random policy, a scripted baseline, and the intended policy through the same wrapper to test logging and metric sanity.
  4. Record failures as structured cases: perception, state, planning, control, task semantics, timing, or metric mismatch.
  5. Report the benchmark's unsupported assumptions beside the positive result.
Simulation Hypothesis Ledger

For Benchmark environment map, a simulator run becomes evidence only after the falsifiable hypothesis, held-out seeds, perturbation panel, and untested real-world assumption are written down.

A common assumption is that a higher-fidelity or more widely adopted simulator automatically produces a more valid benchmark result. It does not. Construct match determines validity, not simulator prestige. A GPU-parallelized contact simulator that omits persistent object state cannot validly measure a household task claim, no matter how fast or accurate its physics engine is. Every benchmark environment covers a specific set of construct features. The only question that matters is whether those features include every requirement of your claim. Everything else is irrelevant.

Leaderboard Trap

A leaderboard score is not automatically a deployment claim. It is evidence for the benchmark's construct, asset distribution, metric, and wrapper version. State that envelope before comparing methods.

Common Pitfall

Consider a specific case: a team trains a pick-and-place policy in MuJoCo, achieves 91% success on the benchmark panel, and then deploys to a real Franka arm. The policy fails on 60% of trials because MuJoCo's default friction model does not reproduce the rubber-tipped gripper pads used on the physical robot. The benchmark never exposed this because all training and evaluation objects shared the same simulated surface coefficient. The score was valid for the benchmark's construct (torque-level control under default friction), but the team's deployment claim required a friction-calibrated construct that the environment never measured. The fix is not to distrust MuJoCo: it is to write a known-limit field in the benchmark card before reporting the score, and to run at least one real-hardware probe with the target object set before asserting transfer.

Practical Example

A mobile manipulation team might use Habitat or ProcTHOR to evaluate navigation and object search, then ManiSkill or Isaac Lab for contact-rich manipulation. The paper-facing claim should not merge those scores into one number. It should say which construct each environment measured and where a real calibration check remains necessary.

Real-World Application: Quadruped Locomotion Deployment

The ANYmal robot team at ETH Zurich trained locomotion policies in Isaac Gym (the GPU-parallelized predecessor of Isaac Lab), then deployed them onto physical hardware that walked construction sites and sewers. They succeeded precisely because the benchmark construct, contact-rich legged dynamics with randomized terrain and actuator models, matched the deployment task, and they explicitly logged the actuator-network gap as a known limit before claiming transfer.

Lab: Measure A Construct-Match Gap Empirically

Goal: see for yourself how a benchmark score can stay high while a real-task feature goes unmeasured, the construct-validity gap in action.

Tools needed: Python, gymnasium, and gymnasium-robotics (pip install gymnasium gymnasium-robotics). Use the FetchPickAndPlace-v2 environment.

Steps: (1) Run a random policy for 200 episodes and record the success rate as your baseline. (2) Train or hand-script a simple reach-and-grasp controller and record its success rate. (3) Now modify the construct: change the object friction or mass in the environment XML (or wrap the env to perturb the reset distribution by widening the object spawn region by 50%).

What to vary: object friction coefficient, spawn-region size, and the success threshold height.

What to observe: how much the success rate moves under each perturbation while the metric script and observation space stay identical. The size of that swing is the slice of construct your original score never controlled for, and it is the gap a real robot would expose. Write each perturbation and its score into a benchmark card so the uncontrolled variable is visible.

Memory Hook

A benchmark is a gym membership for one skill. Winning the treadmill does not prove you can assemble furniture.

Research Frontier

Photorealistic, physics-accurate simulation at scale (2024-2026). NVIDIA Isaac Lab (2024) and ManiSkill3 (Gu et al., 2024) combine GPU-parallelized rigid-body contact solvers with ray-traced rendering (a rendering technique that traces individual light rays to produce photorealistic images and shadows) and paired sensor noise models, enabling thousands of simultaneous rollouts with per-object sim-to-real gap estimates. The frontier is closing the loop between visual realism and contact accuracy in a single environment rather than trading one off against the other.

Foundation-model-driven benchmark generation (2024-2025). RoboGen (Wang et al., 2024) and ManipGen use large language models and diffusion models to synthesize novel task descriptions, object arrangements, and reward functions procedurally, replacing static asset sets with unlimited task variety. This direction targets the asset-diversity bottleneck that causes policies to overfit to the benchmark's fixed object distribution rather than learning generalizable skills.

Standardized evaluation across heterogeneous embodiments (2024-2026). GROOT (2024, NVIDIA) and the AgiBot World dataset (AgiBot, 2025) attempt unified task suites that evaluate the same skill specification across wheeled bases, quadrupeds, and dexterous hands, surfacing how much of a policy's score is embodiment-specific rather than skill-general. The goal is an embodiment-agnostic benchmark card analogous to a language benchmark that scores models regardless of parameter count.

Open problem for a PhD student: structured failure-label taxonomies remain absent from every major benchmark suite. A benchmark can report a 72% success rate but cannot tell you whether the remaining 28% failed due to contact slip, visual misgrounding, state estimation drift, or planning horizon collapse. Building a failure-label protocol that integrates into the Gymnasium reset-step-reward loop, runs at GPU-parallelized speed, and maps each episode's terminal state to a causal category would make benchmark scores diagnostic rather than merely comparative, and would directly unlock the sim-to-real calibration workflows that practitioners currently perform manually and inconsistently.

Self Check

For one benchmark you plan to use, name the construct, observation space, action space, reset distribution, metric, version, and unsupported real-world assumption. If one field is missing, the benchmark choice is not yet defensible.

Naming those seven fields in a self-check is only the first half of the discipline; the second half is binding them into a contract the whole evaluation loop must honor. Benchmark environment map becomes useful when it is tied to a closed-loop contract. The contract names the observation stream, action representation, reset distribution, timing budget, metric, assets, version, and evaluation artifact. Without that contract, a model can look capable in a benchmark table while failing the first time the real task changes an object state the environment never modeled.

The graduate-level habit is to separate three claims. The benchmark claim says what construct the environment measures. The systems claim says what wrapper and artifact make the result reproducible. The transfer claim says which real-world assumptions remain untested. Keeping those claims separate prevents benchmark convenience from becoming benchmark overreach.

Write The Card Before The Policy

A robust implementation starts with a benchmark card before it starts with a policy. The card should log the environment version, wrapper stack, task panel, observation and action spaces, seeds, metric script, asset set, and failure taxonomy. The policy result is interpretable only after that card exists.

Why this matters for embodied AI: a real robot carries physical tolerances, and a simulator approximates those tolerances differently depending on its version, asset set, and reset distribution. Without a written card, two researchers may both call their setups "ManiSkill" yet run different object meshes, friction coefficients, and success thresholds. The gap between their scores then looks identical to a genuine policy difference, so every hardware deployment decision rests on unreliable evidence.

How it works: the card acts as a hash over the environment contract. Each field pins one degree of freedom, so any change yields a different card. Two policies sharing an identical card come from the same frozen world and compare cleanly; any unspecified field is an uncontrolled variable that can mask a real performance difference.

  1. Write a benchmark card with construct, environment version, wrapper stack, seeds, metric, and failure labels.
  2. Run random and scripted baselines to catch broken resets, hidden privileged state, and metric leakage.
  3. Freeze the held-out task panel before tuning policies or curricula.
  4. Save videos, traces, configs, metric outputs, and failure labels in one artifact bundle.
  5. Compare methods only when one script evaluates them on the same benchmark card.
# Build a benchmark card before reporting a policy score.
# The card states what construct the environment can validly measure.
from dataclasses import dataclass, asdict
@dataclass
class BenchmarkCard:
environment: str
construct: str
observation_space: str
action_space: str
metric: str
known_limit: str
def as_row(self) -> dict[str, object]:
return asdict(self)
card = BenchmarkCard(
environment="ManiSkill pick-and-place panel",
construct="vision-conditioned contact manipulation",
observation_space="RGB-D camera plus proprioception",
action_space="end-effector delta pose",
metric="held-out object success rate with failure labels",
known_limit="requires real friction calibration before deployment claims",
)
print(card.as_row())

Code Fragment 9.5.2: This BenchmarkCard defines a ManiSkill-style manipulation panel before any policy score is reported. The card ties the score to a construct, interface, metric, and known real-world limit. RGB-D (Red-Green-Blue plus Depth) denotes a camera that returns a color image alongside a per-pixel depth map.

Expected output: the record exposes the benchmark's construct and its known limit before any model result appears. That ordering keeps the environment from being used to support claims it cannot measure.

Even with the card written first, a policy will sometimes score poorly, and the same construct discipline that selected the environment now tells you how to read that failure. When a benchmark experiment fails, avoid labeling the method as weak before checking the wrapper and construct. First assign the failure to perception, state estimation, planning, control, task semantics, timing, data coverage, or metric mismatch. Then rerun one controlled perturbation that isolates the suspected cause. This pattern turns a disappointing benchmark score into a reusable diagnostic asset.

Key Takeaway

Benchmark environments are useful when their contracts match the construct being claimed and their artifacts make failures diagnosable. Read together, Figure 9.5A's map of simulators by embodiment and task complexity, Figure 9.5B's construct-matching flow, and the benchmark-card habit above are the same map promised at the top of this section: a way to locate, for any claim you want to make, the region of environments that can validly test it.
